Census Data Offers Baselines as ACA Sign Ups Accelarate Across the Country

(Washington, January 1, 2014) Recently released Census Bureau statistics offer a snapshot of the current state of insurance coverage across the country. The Bureau's latests product, the American Community Survey (ACS) includes estimates on a slew of american demographic, health and financial status categories.

One view of the data shows the relationship between rates of poverty by state and percentage of individuals that lack health insurance. With the first round of ACS marketplace coverage going into effect today, Census estimates indicate the rate of uninsured americans remains around 13%, nationwide - that equates to over 40 million people under the age of 65. The rate is as high as 25% in Texas and as low as 4% in Massachusettes, where a sweeping, single payer system was adopted in 2006.

What stands out is the obvious relationship between poverty rate and the uninsured. States like MS, NM and LA maintain rates of both measures well above the national average. Another dozen states follow closely behind. States that buck the trend are generally smaller with state based medicaid expansions in place - examples include VT, NH, WI and MN.

Now, as the ACA moves through its earliest implementations of the new law, policy makers will need to contemplate the relationships between demographics and circumstances for americans across the country.
